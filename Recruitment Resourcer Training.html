Social Care & Housing Sector Overview
Tue 8 Jul · Self-learn pp.9–16 · ~1 hour
SELF-LEARN▶

📌 What to learn

  • Residential care: 24-hour care in a care home. Residents live on-site. Staffing needs are constant — shifts cover day, night and weekends. Workers need experience with personal care.
  • Domiciliary care: Care delivered in the service user's own home. Workers travel between clients throughout the day. Also called "home care." Very high volume of vacancies in this area.
  • Supported living: Individuals live in their own tenancy (often shared) with staff support available. Workers support independence — not the same as full personal care. Increasingly common for LD and mental health service users.
  • Housing sector: Housing associations manage affordable/social housing. They need support workers, floating support staff, and housing officers. The work overlaps significantly with social care.
  • Sector challenges: Chronic staff shortages, high turnover, CQC regulation, and IR35 changes for contractors. Clients need to fill roles quickly and reliably — your speed and accuracy as a Resourcer directly supports this.

🔑 Key Terms

Service user
The person receiving care. Never call them "patient" or "client" in a care context.
Domiciliary care
Care delivered in someone's own home
Supported living
Care model where the person lives independently with on-site or visiting staff support
Housing association
Non-profit organisation that provides affordable/social housing
Floating support
Non-care support (budgeting, tenancy) for people in their own home
CQC
Regulator that inspects and rates care providers. A poor rating = urgent need to hire.

Legislation & Compliance in Social Care Recruitment
Tue–Wed 8–9 Jul · Self-learn pp.17–22 + trainer walkthrough · ~1.5 hours
TRAINED▶

📌 What to learn

  • Right to Work (RTW): You must verify every candidate's right to work in the UK before they start. Accepted documents: passport, biometric residence permit, share code. If in doubt — stop, escalate to your Delivery Consultant.
  • DBS Checks: Disclosure and Barring Service checks screen for criminal records. Basic = any role. Standard = some regulated roles. Enhanced = working with vulnerable adults or children. Enhanced with barred lists = mandatory for direct care work in social care.
  • GDPR in recruitment: You can only store candidate data you have a lawful basis to hold. Candidates must consent to you sharing their CV with clients. You cannot forward a CV without permission. Data must be kept accurate and deleted when no longer needed.
  • Employment Agency Standards (EAS): As a recruitment agency we must not charge candidates fees, must provide key information documents, and must keep accurate records. Your manager will brief you on these obligations.
  • IR35: Relevant for contract (limited company) placements. Determines whether a contractor should be taxed as an employee. For now, flag any contract roles to your Delivery Consultant — don't navigate IR35 alone.
⚠️ Compliance is non-negotiable. Never submit a candidate to a client without first confirming their RTW and DBS status in the CRM. Even if the client is chasing you urgently — if compliance isn't complete, the answer is no.

🔑 Key Terms

RTW
Right to Work — legal check confirming candidate can work in the UK
Enhanced DBS
Highest level of DBS check — required for all direct care/support work
Barred lists
DBS lists of people barred from working with children or vulnerable adults
GDPR
General Data Protection Regulation — governs how candidate data is stored and used
Lawful basis
Legal justification for processing personal data (e.g. consent, legitimate interest)
IR35
Tax legislation affecting contractors — determines employment status for tax purposes

Job Titles & Types of Service in SCH
Wed 9 Jul · Self-learn pp.23–27 · ~45 mins
SELF-LEARN▶

📌 What to learn

  • Support Worker: Entry-level direct care role. Requires compassion, basic personal care skills and ideally an NVQ Level 2 in Health & Social Care (or willingness to work towards it). This is our highest volume vacancy type.
  • Senior Support Worker: More experienced, often responsible for a small team on shift. May have NVQ Level 3. Can deputise for the Team Leader.
  • Team Leader / Shift Leader: Supervises support workers during a shift. Responsible for quality of care, incident reporting, and handovers. Usually NVQ Level 3 minimum.
  • Service Manager / Registered Manager: Runs the whole service. Responsible to CQC. Requires Level 5 Diploma in Leadership for Health & Social Care. These are senior hires — handled primarily by Delivery Consultants but you may source them.
  • Types of service: Mental health supported living, learning disability residential, physical disability domiciliary, autism-specific services, dementia care, elderly residential. Different services need different skills — always check the vacancy spec carefully.
💡 Quick reference: When a client says "we need a Support Worker for our LD supported living service," you know: LD = Learning Disability, the role is entry-level, it's a supported living (not residential) environment, and the candidate needs experience with people who have learning disabilities.

Level 5 Diploma

LD

Learning Disabilities, Autism & Related Conditions
Thu 10 Jul · Self-learn pp.28–32 · ~1 hour
SELF-LEARN▶

📌 What to learn

  • Learning disability vs learning difficulty: A learning disability (e.g. Down's Syndrome) affects intellectual ability and adaptive behaviour. A learning difficulty (e.g. dyslexia) affects specific skills but not overall intelligence. These are different things — don't use them interchangeably with candidates or clients.
  • Autism Spectrum Condition (ASC/ASD): A neurodevelopmental condition affecting social communication and interaction. It can co-exist with a learning disability but is not itself a learning disability. Many people with autism are highly able. Workers need patience, routine, and clear communication.
  • Down's Syndrome: A genetic condition causing some level of learning disability. Candidates working in this area need experience with personal care, communication support and positive behaviour support.
  • ADHD and mental health: Many social care services support people with co-existing conditions — mental health, ADHD, trauma. Workers need trauma-informed approaches and patience.
  • Language matters: Say "person with a learning disability" not "learning disabled person." Say "autistic person" or "person with autism" (check individual preference). Never use outdated or offensive terms — this reflects on Winlight.
💡 Why this matters for sourcing: When screening candidates for LD or autism-specific roles, ask specifically about their experience: "Can you tell me about a time you supported someone with autism?" — vague answers are a red flag. Experienced candidates will give detailed, person-centred responses.

The Resourcer Role: Responsibilities & KPIs
Mon 14 Jul · Trainer-led · ~1.5 hours
TRAINED▶

📌 What to learn

  • The candidate lifecycle: Sourcing → Screening call → CV preparation → Submission to client → Interview arranged → Offer → Compliance → Start. You own every step from sourcing through to submission. The Delivery Consultant takes it from there.
  • Your KPIs: You will be measured on daily call volume, new candidates registered per week, CVs submitted, interview conversion rate, and — eventually — placements. These are your targets. Track them daily.
  • Prioritising vacancies: Not all vacancies are equal. A temp shift starting tomorrow is more urgent than a perm role with a 4-week notice period. Your Delivery Consultant will brief you on priority order each morning — ask if you're unsure.
  • Time ownership: A good Resourcer plans their day the night before. First 30 mins = admin and callbacks. Core hours = sourcing and calling. End of day = CRM updates and tomorrow's prep.
  • Communication with the DC: Brief your Delivery Consultant on interesting candidates proactively — don't wait to be asked. "I've spoken to someone who could be great for X — want me to write them up?" is music to a DC's ears.

CRM System & Job Board Sourcing
Mon–Tue 14–15 Jul · Trainer-led · ~3 hours
TRAINED▶

📌 What to learn

  • CRM navigation: How to search the existing candidate database — always check here first before going to job boards. You may already have the right person. Log every call, every note, every status change.
  • Adding candidates: Full name, contact details, current role, availability, DBS status, RTW status. Incomplete records are useless. Quality over speed.
  • Job boards — CV Library, Reed, Indeed: Each has different strengths. CV Library is strong for care sector candidates. Reed has good coverage. Indeed has high volume but more noise. Your trainer will show you the accounts.
  • Boolean search: The most powerful sourcing tool. Use AND to narrow results, OR to broaden, NOT to exclude. Brackets group terms. Example: ("support worker" OR "care worker") AND ("learning disability" OR "LD") AND London NOT manager
  • Compliance in the CRM: Every candidate profile must show RTW checked, DBS type and status before a submission is logged. This protects Winlight legally — it's not optional.
💡 Boolean tip: Put exact job titles in speech marks. "Support worker" (with quotes) finds that exact phrase. Without quotes, the search engine may return anything containing "support" or "worker" separately — far too broad.

Organisational Skills & Time Management
Thu 17 Jul · Trainer-led · ~1 hour
TRAINED▶

📌 What to learn

  • The Urgent/Important Matrix: Quadrant 1 = Urgent & Important (do now). Quadrant 2 = Important, not urgent (schedule it). Quadrant 3 = Urgent, not important (delegate or batch). Quadrant 4 = Neither (eliminate). Most Resourcers spend too much time in Q3 — be disciplined.
  • Managing multiple vacancies: Create a simple daily priority list. Which vacancy has the hardest deadline? Which client is most important to the DC? Focus your best calling hours on those first.
  • Your daily rhythm: Morning — review your call-back list and reply to any overnight emails. Mid-morning to 1pm — peak calling time (candidates are reachable). Afternoon — sourcing, CRM admin, writing CVs. End of day — update CRM and prep tomorrow's list.
  • Outlook Calendar: Block time for calls. Set reminders for candidate callbacks. Use recurring blocks for daily CRM updates. Share your calendar with your DC so they know when you're in a meeting.
  • OneNote: Keep a running note per vacancy. Capture names, numbers, call notes and any context the DC has given you. This is your personal working file — keep it tidy.
💡 Peak calling hours: 8–9am and 5–6.30pm are when candidates are most reachable (before and after work). Block these in your calendar as sacred sourcing time. Mid-afternoon is best for admin.

WhatsApp Business — Candidate Follow-Up
Fri 18 Jul · Trainer-led · ~45 mins
TRAINED▶

📌 What to learn

  • Why WhatsApp Business: Many care sector candidates don't answer calls from unknown numbers but will respond to a WhatsApp. Response rates are significantly higher than phone or email for initial contact.
  • Setup: Use the Winlight business WhatsApp account (your trainer will set you up). Never use your personal WhatsApp number for candidate contact — this creates GDPR and handover issues.
  • Quick replies: Set up quick replies for your most common messages (initial outreach, availability check, interview confirmation). Saves huge amounts of time at scale.
  • The 3-touch follow-up: Touch 1 = WhatsApp message + call attempt. Touch 2 (24 hours later) = follow-up WhatsApp. Touch 3 (48 hours after Touch 2) = final message stating you'll close the file. After 3 touches with no response, move on and log it in the CRM.
  • GDPR: You can only message candidates who have given consent (e.g. by applying, registering, or giving permission). Don't cold-message people who haven't engaged with you. When a candidate asks to be removed — do it immediately and log it.
💡 3-touch example:
Touch 1: "Hi [Name], I'm Chhavi from Winlight Recruitment — I came across your profile and think I may have a great opportunity for you in [area]. Are you open to hearing more? 😊"
Touch 2 (24hrs): "Hi again — just following up on my message yesterday. Happy to jump on a quick call at a time that suits you?"
Touch 3 (48hrs later): "Hi [Name] — I'll leave it here for now, but please do get in touch if you're ever open to new roles. Best wishes."

📌 How this works

Role Play: Handling Objections & Unresponsive Candidates
Wed 23 Jul · Role play activity · ~2 hours
ROLE PLAY▶

📌 Scenarios to practise

  • Scenario 1 — "I'm not actively looking": Don't argue or push. Ask: "That's completely fine — out of curiosity, what would need to change in your current role for you to consider a move?" Listen. You're planting a seed, not closing a deal today.
  • Scenario 2 — "I already have an interview elsewhere": Great news — they're clearly open to moving. Say: "That's brilliant, hopefully that goes well. While you're exploring options, it's always worth having a conversation — this role has [X advantage]. Can I tell you about it quickly?" Keep it brief.
  • Scenario 3 — No response after initial contact: Execute the 3-touch WhatsApp follow-up. After 3 touches, log it and move on. Never send a 4th message — it damages the brand.
  • Scenario 4 — "What's the salary?": If you don't have the exact figure yet: "The salary is competitive for the sector — I'm confirming the exact figure with the client today and will come back to you. In the meantime, what's your current package?" Redirect to gather information.
  • Scenario 5 — Candidate withdraws after submission: Stay professional — this happens. Inform the DC immediately. Ask the candidate what changed — you may be able to resolve it or use the intel for future. Log everything in the CRM.
💡 Role play tip: Ask your trainer to make it difficult — give you a rude candidate, a last-minute withdrawal, an unexpected question. The more uncomfortable it feels in role play, the more comfortable you'll be when it happens for real.

Writing & Submitting CVs to Clients
Thu 24 Jul · Trainer-led · ~2 hours
TRAINED▶

📌 What to learn

  • Anonymising CVs: Remove the candidate's name, address, and contact details before sending to a client. They should only see skills, experience and qualifications. Winlight's name goes on the covering email — this protects the candidate from being contacted directly.
  • Formatting to Winlight standard: Use the CV template your trainer provides. Consistent formatting makes us look professional and makes it easy for clients to compare candidates.
  • The cover note: One paragraph per CV telling the client why this specific person fits this specific role. Don't repeat the CV — add context: "Chloe has 3 years of LD residential experience and is available from Monday. She is based 10 minutes from your Bristol service." Specific, relevant, brief.
  • Compliance check before submission: RTW confirmed? DBS type confirmed, status tracked? Candidate consent given? If any of these are missing — do not submit. Log the reason in the CRM and resolve it.
  • The submission email: CC your DC. Subject line format: [SUBMISSION] – [Role] – [Client] – [Candidate initials]. Body: brief intro + cover note per candidate + attach formatted CVs. Acknowledge any specific requirements the client mentioned.

Escalation Process — When to Go to Your DC
Thu 24 Jul · Trainer-led · ~45 mins
TRAINED▶

📌 What to escalate — and how

  • Always escalate: Compliance issues (missing RTW or DBS), a candidate raising a safeguarding concern, a client making an unreasonable or discriminatory request, any threat or abusive behaviour from a candidate.
  • Escalate quickly: A candidate withdrawing on the same day they're due to start, a client rejecting a submission without feedback (the DC needs to find out why), salary negotiation conversations.
  • How to brief your DC: Be concise. "I have a situation — Candidate X has just called and said they won't be starting tomorrow. They gave [reason]. I've logged it in the CRM. What do you want to do?" Don't bring the problem without having logged it first.
  • Don't escalate unnecessarily: If a candidate doesn't answer the phone, that's not an escalation. Use your judgment — ask yourself: "Can I resolve this myself within my remit?" If yes, do it and log it. If no, escalate.
